Bloom App

A mobile wellness app built on behavior change research.

RoleLead UX Designer
Timeline10 Weeks
Team1 Designer · 2 Engineers · 1 PM
ToolsFigma · Maze · Miro
Problem

People want to build healthy habits — but most apps make it harder, not easier.

Most wellness apps overwhelm users with tracking, streaks, and guilt mechanics. Research showed that users would download a new app, engage for 3–5 days, then abandon it entirely. The challenge: design an experience that builds lasting habits without creating pressure or shame.

User Challenge: Users felt overwhelmed by all-or-nothing habit mechanics and lost motivation after missing a day.
Business Challenge: Retention beyond 7 days was under 12%. The product needed to differentiate on emotional design.
Research

Understanding the dropout cycle.

I conducted 8 user interviews with adults aged 24–40 who had tried and abandoned at least two wellness apps. I also ran a competitive analysis of 6 leading apps and synthesized findings into personas and journey maps.

Affinity Map — User Interview Synthesis

Journey Map — Habit Dropout Cycle

Key finding: Users didn't want more tracking. They wanted gentle encouragement and flexibility — the ability to have a "small win" even on a bad day.

Design Process

From concepts to validated flows.

I ran a design sprint across 5 days, producing low-fidelity wireframes for 3 competing concepts. After usability testing with 5 participants, I converged on a "gentle momentum" model — where any positive action counted, no matter how small.

Mid-Fi Prototype — Onboarding

Final Solution

A flexible, compassionate experience.

The final design introduced "micro-habits" — users could log even a 2-minute walk as a win. The app used warm, non-judgmental language and removed streaks entirely in favor of a "momentum score" that reset softly, not harshly.

Results & Impact

Measurable improvement in retention.

+64%

Day-7 retention in beta testing

4.7★

Users said the app felt "supportive, not stressful"

Reflection

What I learned.

This project reinforced that the best UX often means removing features, not adding them. The decision to cut streaks was controversial internally but proved critical to user trust. In the future, I'd push for live A/B testing to validate the momentum model with real behavioral data, not just self-reported satisfaction.
